# Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ) about NAS

**Q: What is the purpose of NAS?**
A: NAS aims to provide a simple and universal way to represent name structures and individual preferences for address, fostering understanding and respect for name diversity.

**Q: How do I know what my NAS notation is?**
A: Determine the order of your given names and surnames. Use 'n' for each given name unit and 's' for each surname unit, separated by a point. Capitalize the 'N' and 'S' corresponding to the name(s) you prefer to be addressed by. Use a hyphen '-' to connect parts of a single name unit if needed.

**Q: Where should I use NAS?**
A: NAS can be helpful in formal settings like email signatures, publications, conference materials, and online profiles where clarity about your name structure and preferences is beneficial.

**Q: Is NAS mandatory?**
A: No, NAS is a voluntary system intended to help those who wish to clarify their name structure and preferences.

**Q: Do I have just one NAS?**
A: No — and this is central to v.2. A name and the way you want it acknowledged are deeply personal, and they can legitimately differ by context. You might use a formal rendering for legal documents or publications (e.g. Paulina A. Valenzuela Lagos **(N.n.S.S)**) and a casual one for everyday email (e.g. Paulina Valenzuela Lagos **(N.(n.)S.s)**). Keeping several renderings is expected, not a contradiction.

**Q: What do the parentheses inside a NAS mean, like (n.)?**
A: Parentheses mark an **implied** unit: the name exists and you're telling the reader so, but you're choosing not to write it in that rendering. In **(N.(n.)S.s)**, the `(n.)` says "there is another given name here" — the reader knows it's there without seeing it.

**Q: My name has no family surname (e.g. Icelandic or a patronymic). How do I write it?**
A: v.2 adds optional **`p`** (patronymic) and **`m`** (matronymic) for units derived from a parent's given name that are not inherited family names — for example Björk Guðmundsdóttir **(N.p)**. You may still use `s` if you prefer; the extensions just avoid implying a family name that isn't there.

**Q: Can I write a NAS notation for someone else, or guess one if I don't know their preference?**
A: No. NAS is strictly **self-assigned** — it is only authoritative when published by the person it describes (their website, business card, email signature, author bio). If you don't know someone's NAS, use their name as given, or ask them; don't construct one on their behalf. This is the intended norm, though mistakes will still happen in practice.

**Q: Does NAS track my name history — e.g. after marriage, gender transition, or a legal name change?**
A: No, and it's not meant to. NAS is **present-tense**, the same way pronouns (he/she/they) or honorifics (Miss/Mrs/Mr/Mx) are — it states how you want to be addressed *now*, not a record of who you used to be. If your name changes, your NAS simply changes with it; nothing about the previous version needs to be shown or implied.

**Q: Does NAS help with pronouncing names correctly?**
A: No — that's deliberately out of scope. Pronunciation is a distinct, substantial linguistic problem on its own. NAS only addresses how a name is *structured and written* for formal and semi-formal address.

**Q: Why does NAS use English letters (n, s, N, S, p, m) for its symbols?**
A: For practicality, not preference. English currently works as a lingua franca across cultures and disciplines, which makes it a convenient shared notation — it isn't a claim that Western naming conventions are the "default." The names themselves can be written in any script.

**Q: What if I have a very complex name structure?**
A: NAS tries to be adaptable. Use the hyphen '-' and double hyphen '--' to represent combined or longer units. If you encounter a structure that doesn't seem to fit, feel free to suggest improvements!

**Q: Who developed NAS?**

**Q: How can I contribute to NAS?**
A: Suggestions and comments are welcome! Please feel free to open an issue or submit a pull request on our GitHub repository.
