## Introduction: Recognizing and Valuing Name Diversity

Personal names are fundamental to identity, yet naming conventions vary dramatically across cultures and languages. The often-unspoken assumption of a universal Western first-middle-last name structure can lead to misunderstandings and a lack of respect for individual preferences.

The **Names Acknowledgment System (NAS)** offers a simple and adaptable framework to clearly represent name structures and individual preferences for address, promoting greater understanding and respect in all interactions.

## A Name Is Personal — and So Is Its NAS

> **This is the core idea of v.2:** a person's name, and the way they want it acknowledged, is deeply personal. There is no single "correct" NAS for a person. The *same* individual may keep **several renderings** and choose whichever fits the moment.

The way you present your name in a courtroom, a scientific journal, a passport, and a message to a friend can all legitimately differ — and each of those can have its own NAS. NAS does not tell you who someone *is*; it records how they would like to be **acknowledged in a given context**.

### One person, many renderings

Take **Paulina Andrea Valenzuela Lagos**. She might use:

* **Legal document / scientific publication**
  * *Paulina A. Valenzuela Lagos* **(N.n.S.S)**
  * Full formality: the preferred given name *Paulina* (`N`), the second given name *Andrea* still present but abbreviated to an initial "A." (`n`), and **both** surnames emphasized (`S.S`).

* **Daily / casual email — two valid renderings, depending on what's actually written**
  * *Paulina Valenzuela Lagos* **(N.(n.)S.s)** — here *Lagos* is written in the text, so it's marked lowercase `s`: present, not required for address.
  * *Paulina Valenzuela* **(N.(n.)S.(s.))** — this is what she actually signs emails with. *Lagos* is **not** written at all, so it takes the parenthetical `(s.)` — implied, not merely de-emphasized.
  * In both, *Andrea* is **implied but not written** — the parentheses `(n.)` tell the reader "there is another given name here" without displaying it. *Valenzuela* is the preferred surname (`S`) either way.

Same person. Same underlying name. Two honest, self-chosen acknowledgments — the difference between them is simply what's on the page.

> **Consistency rule:** a unit's notation must match what's actually written next to it. `N`/`n` or `S`/`s` means the unit *appears* in the text (capitalization only marks preference); `(n)`/`(s)` means the unit is *absent* from the text. This is what lets a reader recover, from the notation alone, whether to expect a given unit in the name string above it.

## Guiding Principles

* **Self-assigned, never inferred.** A NAS notation is only authoritative when the person it describes has published it themselves — on their own website, business card, email signature, or author bio. If you don't know someone's NAS, use their name as given, or ask; don't construct or guess one on their behalf. Mistakes will still happen, but this is the standard NAS aims for.

* **Present-tense, not historical.** Like pronouns (he/she/they) or honorifics (Miss/Mrs/Mr/Mx), NAS states how a person wants to be addressed *right now*. It is not a record of former names, marital-status changes, or the reasoning behind a surname's position — for example, Chile's 2022 change allowing a person's maternal surname to be listed before the paternal one changes nothing about how NAS is written; it simply reflects the current order. If Alexa Apple becomes Alexa Smith, her NAS reflects Alexa Smith — no trace of Apple required.

* **Scope: written formal and semi-formal address.** NAS structures how a name is written and addressed in text. It deliberately does not address **pronunciation** — a distinct and substantial linguistic problem in its own right, and out of scope here.

* **A pragmatic, not ideological, use of English.** NAS's symbols (`n`, `s`, `N`, `S`, `p`, `m`, `t`) are written with English letters because English currently serves as a lingua franca across cultures and disciplines — a practical choice for a shared tool, not a claim that Western naming logic is the default. The names themselves may be written in any script.

* **Easy to type, not a private code.** NAS deliberately uses characters already on every keyboard — letters, dots, parentheses, hyphens — so anyone can write their own notation without special symbols or tools. The tradeoff: until NAS is more widely recognized, a reader unfamiliar with it may mistake the parenthetical for something else (a nickname, pronouns, a maiden name). That's an accepted cost of keeping the notation approachable rather than inventing new symbols to make it unambiguous.

## Core Components of NAS Notation

The NAS notation is placed in parentheses immediately following a person's name:

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **n** | given name unit (present, not the preferred address form) |
| **s** | surname / family-name unit (present, not the preferred address form) |
| **N** | **preferred** given name(s) — or the initial(s) of the preferred given name(s) |
| **S** | **preferred** surname(s) |
| **.** | separator between distinct units of a name |
| **-** | connects parts of a *single* name/surname unit treated as one (multi-character given names, hyphenated names) |
| **--** | a longer multi-part unit acting as a single component |
| **t** | title or honorific (used when relevant to formal identification) |

### New in v.2 — Rendering & context

| Symbol | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **( … )** | **implied / omitted unit** — the name exists and the reader is told so, but it is not written in *this* rendering. Example: `(n.)` = "a given name is here, intentionally not shown." |
| **p** | **patronymic** unit — derived from a parent's (usually father's) given name; occupies the surname slot but is *not* an inherited family name (see [Extended structures](#extended-structures-patronymics-and-matronymics)). |
| **m** | **matronymic** unit — same, derived from the mother's given name. |

> `p` and `m` are **optional extended components.** If you prefer, you may keep using `s` for any trailing unit — the extensions exist to honour structures that a family-name assumption would misrepresent.

### The three rendering states

Every unit is in one of three states, which is the heart of v.2:

1. **Preferred** — `N` / `S` — emphasized; use this to address the person. The unit is written.
2. **Present** — `n` / `s` — part of the written name, but not required for address. May be written in full *or* abbreviated to an initial (e.g. "A."). The unit is written.
3. **Implied** — `(n)` / `(s)` — acknowledged to exist, but the unit is **not** written.

Each state tells you, unambiguously, whether the unit appears in the text next to the notation — see the [consistency rule](#one-person-many-renderings) above.

## Understanding NAS: Structure and Preference

* **Basic Structure:** Lowercase `n` and `s` separated by points delineate the sequence of given names and surnames.
  * Example: Paulina Andrea Valenzuela Lagos **(n.n.s.s)**

* **Indicating Preference:** Capitalizing `N` and `S` signifies the name(s) the individual prefers to be addressed by.
  * Example: Paulina Andrea Valenzuela Lagos **(N.n.S.S)** (Prefers "Paulina" and both surnames)
  * Example: John David Smith **(N.n.S)** (Prefers "John Smith")
  * Example: Nai Yun Blanca Hsiao **(N-.n.S)** (Prefers "Nai Yun" and "Hsiao")
  * Example: Nguyễn Thao Thách Minh **(S.N.n.n)** (Prefers "Nguyễn" and "Thao")

* **Combined Name Units:** The hyphen `-` groups parts of a name that function as a single unit.
  * Example: Eun-jung Lee **(n-.s)**
  * Example: 范寶琳 **(s.n-)** (Surname with one character, given name with two)

* **Extended Name Units:** The double hyphen `--` signifies a longer, multi-part component treated as one unit.
  * Example: 小泉 純一郎 **(s-.n--)**

* **Titles:** `t` denotes a title, with capitalization showing preferred usage.
  * Example: King Charles Philip Arthur George **(t.N.n.n.n)**

## Extended structures: patronymics and matronymics

Some naming systems have no inherited family name at all. Treating those units as `s` quietly imposes a Western assumption, so v.2 offers `p` / `m`.

* **Icelandic** — people carry a given name plus a patronymic (father's name + *-son* / *-dóttir*), are addressed by their **given name**, and are alphabetized by it. The patronymic is not a shared family surname.
  * Björk Guðmundsdóttir **(N.p)** — addressed as "Björk"; *Guðmundsdóttir* = "daughter of Guðmundur."
  * Guðni Th. Jóhannesson **(N.n.p)** — middle name *Thorlacius* shown as the initial "Th.".

* **Russian** — given name + patronymic + family name; formal address is *given name + patronymic*.
  * Vladimir Vladimirovich Putin **(N.P.s)** (formal — "Vladimir Vladimirovich")
  * Vladimir Vladimirovich Putin **(N.p.s)** (casual — "Vladimir")

* **Ethiopian** — a given name followed by the father's given name; there is no family name.
  * Abiy Ahmed **(N.p)** — addressed as "Abiy"; *Ahmed* is the father's given name.

## Practical Application of NAS

Integrating NAS can improve communication and respect in various settings. Remember that you may present a **different rendering in each**:

* **Email Signatures:** include the *casual* rendering you're comfortable with.
  * Example:
    ```
    Paulina Valenzuela (N.(n.)S.(s.))
    CEO of NAS
    ```

* **Publications & legal identification:** use your *formal* rendering.
  * Example: Paulina A. Valenzuela Lagos (N.n.S.S)¹, …

* **Conferences / Events:** offer an optional NAS field on registration materials, and let people give more than one.

* **It's static, and entirely optional.** The badge is a decorative recognizability aid, not a new way of encoding a name. Writing the plain text `(N.n.S.S)` by hand is fully valid and authoritative on its own, with or without the image next to it.
* **The corner is the spec version, not a personal version.** "v.2" identifies which revision of the *NAS notation itself* is in use, so a badge someone downloaded today stays correctly interpretable even if the spec adds symbols later. It has nothing to do with how many times a person has updated their own name or preferences.
* **It's script-agnostic.** The badge never renders the person's name — it's a generic marker that sits next to *any* name, in *any* alphabet, unchanged.

## Respectful Address Using NAS

When you see a name with NAS notation:

* Only trust a NAS notation **published by the person it describes** (their website, business card, email signature, byline). Don't construct one for someone else.
* Prioritize the units indicated by **capitalization** (`N` and `S` / `P`).
* Treat units in **parentheses** as private — acknowledged but deliberately not displayed; don't surface them unprompted.
* In general conversation, using the preferred given name(s) (`N`) is usually appropriate.
* In formal contexts or when unsure, use the preferred given name(s) followed by the preferred surname(s), matching the rendering the person gave you for that context.

## Known Limitations

* **Accessibility.** The core preference signal in NAS is capitalization (`N`/`n`, `S`/`s`). Most screen readers don't announce case differences in normal reading mode, so a blind or low-vision user may not receive the preference signal without switching to character-spelling mode — a degraded experience compared to what a sighted reader gets at a glance. This is a real gap for a system aimed at respectful address, and it's open for future improvement rather than solved today.
